Branding cloud.gov

Creating a brand for a government-owned cloud platform

Background

cloud.gov is a Platform as a Service (PaaS) owned and operated by the U.S. General Services Administration’s Technology Transformation Service (TTS).

cloud.gov’s funding mechanism means it is an experiment that needed to be cost recoverable, so the ability to market its advantages to other government agencies is paramount.

The problem

When I joined the team in fall 2015, the sum total of material available to market the product was 5,000 words of long-hand Q&A and talking points for our outreach and communications teams. We also had the expertise of the person who instigated the development of cloud.gov in the first place, but unless you could navigate a command line, you could not learn for yourself what cloud.gov was all about. We needed government CIOs, product engineers, and compliance teams to understand why a sales conversation was worth their time.

The work

5,000 words of longhand writing can’t give a product identity, but they can—and in this case did—provide the raw materials of one. I started by breaking the series of questions down into a categorized content inventory. I captured light metadata about each one: Did it address security, cloud infrastructure, price, or what? Who were the audiences for the information in any given question?

The most content-dense categories of information told me what we wanted to say about the product, while the alignment of audiences against those categories helped me understand who we were ready to talk to. With this, I broke the content into sections where question categories with similar audience alignment could be grouped together to enable coherent reading. I forefronted content aimed at audiences we were most prepared for (managers of engineering teams rather than information security officers). And because the technical knowledge of government technology managers is a mixed bag, I emphasized a collegial rather than technical tone.

This was still not a sales pitch, however. Turning dense information into a homepage that would help CIOs see how it would benefit their teams to use cloud.gov meant telling the right story. Our team’s visual designer and I captured the content and visual choices of vendors in the space. We looked to be similar on claims of credibility and ease of use and different on claims of focus (in our case, built by and for the government’s particular security and compliance needs, without sacrificing functionality).

The differentiation exercise let us explore two homepage directions: 1) a high-level short narrative of why the product exists and why people should be confident in it, and 2) a focus on specific advantages and features. We worked each one through the visual direction we were going and made a decision.

The results

cloud.gov’s initial direction gave the entire team a common story about the product, one that aligned the sales, product, and engineerng teams with the goals of the platform’s creator. A year later, when I returned to the team to do additional work, the clarity of the initial direction made testing it for further improvement far easier than it otherwise might have been. It was decidedly flawed, but it facilitated strong usability research and iteration.
