How to apply for a job at the CFPB

Making government hiring understandable to people who haven’t been there

Background

The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) was created in the wake of the financial crisis to to provide a single point of accountability for enforcing federal consumer financial laws and protecting consumers in the financial marketplace. In 2015, I was working on a team redesigning the agency’s website, consumerfinance.gov. As my team moved into redsigning the careers section of the site, we encountered an opportunity.

One of the CFPB’s operating principles was a commitment to bringing the brightest minds it could find into government to pursue a meaningful mission. In a number of different areas, the agency encountered problems supporting this commitment. Recruiting technologists to move to the capital was difficult. The pace of work across the agency was grueling. And for some roles, the very companies we were regulating was willing to pay far more money to join them.

The result was that hiring was a constant need. The agency couldn’t afford to have applications from good people rejected because they didn’t understand government hiring rules.

The problem

Government hiring rules are complex. Even within a single job within a single agency, there maybe several different sets of standards for how to qualify. This makes it hard to provide clear and accurate information to the public, who generally aren’t familiar with the basics let alone the specifics. But these hard-to-understand rules are strictly observed. Being rejected as unqualified from a job opportunity for which anyone can see you are, in fact, qualified is not uncommon.

The CFPB’s mission was all about making the fine print understandable to ordinary consumers. As a matter of the organization’s ethos, it was unacceptable to decide the complexity of rules meant we couldn’t offer direct, useful advice.

The work

I worked with the Director of Talent Acquisition to identify the few parts of the application process that stood in the way most often. Rather than trying to clear the entire set of hurdles, we focused on the ones that seem most opaque and cause the most mysterious frustration to job applicants. We settled on three core issues: the concept of “minimally qualified” the unpredictability of the hiring manager’s role in the process, and the right way to ask a direct question about your own application.

The deeper we got into explaining the process, the clearer it became that we couldn’t write a set of style-compliant web content that could make the process transparent enough to work through. What we needed was to tell a story that let people have a lightbulb moment about their own experience, whether they had been rejected or were trying to figure it out for the first time.

I wrote a script and worked with our visual designers to develop a tone, vocabulary, and visual language that served to demystify. We focused on approachability and giving applicants a clear understanding of how and why the process feels weird.

Results

The fist thing the video accomplished was to set down a marker for the website we were redesigning. It was released more than a year before the redesign changeover from beta.consumerfinance.gov to consumerfinance.gov and it established both the voice and visual direction we were giving the new site.

The impact on the CFPB’s hiring efforts of the video itself was never well ascertained. The growing number of modern digital programs in the federal government, however, have used this video to encourage assertive recruitment by their own agencies. Agencies often give into their own worries that innovative people don’t apply, and the most effective thing to do sometimes is to arm people who are trying to change their minds with tools like this.

The video in this case study supports the CFPB’s introduction to the job application process.
